
# BASIC GREEK VERBS TRACK

## INTRODUCTION

Welcome to the Greek Verbs Track.

This course is designed to help complete beginners understand Greek verbs progressively and confidently. The flow is inspired by the educational structure of Bill Mounce while adapting the lessons for a modern interactive learning environment.

The purpose of this course is not merely to memorize labels.

The goal is to understand how Greek communicates meaning.

---

# LESSON 1 — WHAT VERBS ARE IN ENGLISH

## Big Idea

Before learning Greek verbs, we first need to understand what verbs are in normal language.

A verb communicates:
- action
- condition
- existence
- process
- experience

Examples:
- run
- speak
- believe
- know
- is

Without verbs, sentences lose movement and meaning.

## Why This Matters

Greek verbs can feel intimidating at first because they carry a lot of information.

But underneath the Greek endings and labels is the same basic idea:
verbs communicate what is happening.

## Mini Activity

Identify the verb:
1. The teacher speaks.
2. God is faithful.
3. The disciples walked.

Answers:
1. speaks
2. is
3. walked

## Takeaway

Verbs are the engine of sentences.

---

# LESSON 2 — SUBJECTS AND ACTIONS

## Big Idea

Verbs connect to subjects.

The subject performs or experiences the action.

Examples:
- The student learns.
- The crowd listened.
- Jesus spoke.

## Key Concept

Greek often communicates the subject through the verb ending itself.

This becomes important later.

## Activity

Identify the subject and verb:
“The disciple writes.”

Subject:
disciple

Verb:
writes

---

# LESSON 3 — TIME AND ACTION

## Big Idea

English verbs often communicate time:
- present
- past
- future

Examples:
- writes
- wrote
- will write

Greek also communicates how actions are viewed.

This idea becomes important when discussing aspect.

## Common Mistake

Do not reduce Greek tense to ONLY time.

Greek often communicates viewpoint as well.

---

# LESSON 4 — COMMANDS AND STATEMENTS

## Big Idea

Not every sentence communicates the same kind of idea.

Examples:
- “He speaks.” (statement)
- “Speak!” (command)
- “If he speaks…” (possibility/condition)

Greek has formal systems describing these kinds of communication.

---

# LESSON 5 — INTRO TO GREEK VERBS

## Big Idea

Greek verbs often contain:
- person
- number
- tense
- voice
- mood

inside the verb itself.

## Example

γράφω

Translation:
“I write”

This single word already communicates:
- first person
- singular
- present
- active
- indicative

Later lessons will explain each category carefully.

---

# LESSON 6 — PERSON AND NUMBER

## Person

Greek verbs communicate:
- first person
- second person
- third person

## Number

Greek distinguishes:
- singular
- plural

## Examples

γράφω — I write  
γράφεις — you write  
γράφει — he/she/it writes

---

# LESSON 7 — PRESENT ACTIVE INDICATIVE

## Big Idea

Most Greek textbooks begin with the present active indicative because it forms the foundation of many verb discussions.

## Present

The action is viewed as ongoing or in progress.

## Active

The subject performs the action.

## Indicative

The sentence communicates assertion or statement.

## Example

λύω

“I loose” or “I destroy”

---

# LESSON 8 — UNDERSTANDING ENDINGS

## Big Idea

Greek endings communicate meaning.

The stem usually carries the core meaning.
The ending carries grammatical information.

Example:

γράφ-ω

Stem:
γράφ-

Ending:
-ω

---

# LESSON 9 — ACTIVE VOICE

## Big Idea

In active voice, the subject performs the action.

Examples:
- The teacher teaches.
- The student learns.

Greek Example:
γράφει

“He/she writes.”

---

# LESSON 10 — MIDDLE AND PASSIVE BASICS

## Passive Voice

The subject receives the action.

Example:
“The letter was written.”

## Middle Voice

The subject is connected to or involved in the action in some way.

Important:
Do not oversimplify the middle voice.

---

# LESSON 11 — INDICATIVE MOOD

## Big Idea

The indicative mood typically communicates assertion or presentation of reality.

Examples:
- He speaks.
- They traveled.
- We believe.

---

# LESSON 12 — IMPERFECT TENSE

## Big Idea

The imperfect often communicates past ongoing action.

Example:
“He was teaching.”

Greek writers often use the imperfect in narrative sections.

---

# LESSON 13 — FUTURE TENSE

## Big Idea

The future tense commonly communicates future action or expectation.

Example:
“He will speak.”

---

# LESSON 14 — AORIST BASICS

## Big Idea

The aorist is one of the most important Greek verb systems.

It often presents action as a whole or undefined event.

Important:
Avoid simplistic explanations like:
“once-for-all action.”

The aorist is more nuanced than that.

---

# LESSON 15 — SECOND AORISTS

## Big Idea

Second aorists use different stems but generally function similarly to first aorists.

Students should focus on recognition.

---

# LESSON 16 — PERFECT TENSE

## Big Idea

The perfect tense often communicates a completed action with continuing result.

Example idea:
“It has been written.”

---

# LESSON 17 — IMPERATIVES

## Big Idea

Imperatives communicate commands.

Examples:
- Go!
- Listen!
- Write!

---

# LESSON 18 — INFINITIVES

## Big Idea

Infinitives are verbal forms often functioning similarly to verbal nouns.

English examples:
- to speak
- to learn
- to write

---

# LESSON 19 — PARTICIPLES

## Big Idea

Participles combine verbal and adjectival ideas.

They are extremely common in NT Greek.

---

# LESSON 20 — PRINCIPAL PARTS

## Big Idea

Principal parts help students recognize different verb systems.

Memorizing principal parts greatly improves reading ability.

---

# LESSON 21 — READING FULL PARSES

Students now begin combining:
- tense
- voice
- mood
- person
- number

into full parsing recognition.

---

# LESSON 22 — READING REAL NT VERBS

Students begin:
- identifying verbs in context
- recognizing systems naturally
- following Greek sentence flow
